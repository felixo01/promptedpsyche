A good argument often sounds strongest to the person who has just built it. AI can help show where a claim is weaker, where it depends on assumptions and what could change the conclusion. The point is not to win an argument against the model. The point is to stress-test your own thinking.

This practice is useful when you have an idea, decision, text or position and want to see how it could be challenged fairly.

## When this helps

- When you are preparing an argument, article, presentation or decision.
- When a claim sounds good, but you are not sure where it is weak.
- When you want a serious counterargument, not polite criticism.
- When you want to separate defending a position from checking it.

## What to ask the model

Ask the model for the strongest fair counterargument, not a rhetorical fight. The model should show tensions, assumptions and conditions that could change your view.

```text
Read my argument and treat it as a working claim, not as finished truth.

Help me test it in 6 steps:

1. Formulate the strongest fair counterargument.
2. Identify which parts of my argument are weakest.
3. List the assumptions the counterargument depends on.
4. Identify what evidence or examples could weaken my position.
5. Identify what evidence or examples could strengthen it.
6. Suggest 3 questions I should ask myself before using the argument further.

Do not treat yourself as the judge. Do not try to win the debate. Help me see the conditions under which my claim is strong or weak.

Argument:
[paste argument]
```

## What to check yourself

- Whether the counterargument addresses the actual claim, not an easier version of it.
- Whether the model makes the opposing side too strong just to sound critical.
- Whether the weak points follow from the material or from extra assumptions.
- Whether you know what sources, data or experience could change the assessment.
- Whether the argument becomes more precise, not just more defensive.

## What can go wrong

- The model may create a counterargument that sounds strong but misses the main issue.
- The user may treat the model's answer as a verdict.
- The exercise may become a rhetorical game instead of a check on thinking.
- An overly aggressive counterargument may hide what is still valuable in the original claim.

## Better way to use the answer

You write that AI can help teams organize information faster. The model gives a counterargument: faster organization may increase trust in weakly checked conclusions. The better move is not to abandon the original claim. It is to add a condition: AI helps if the team has checking criteria, decision ownership and time for verification.

This kind of counterargument supports [calibrated trust](/concepts/calibrated-trust/). The model does not decide which side is right, but it can help show what the argument depends on and where [epistemic vigilance](/concepts/epistemic-vigilance/) is needed.

## Short rule

Do not ask AI to defeat your argument. Ask it to help you see when the argument stops being strong enough.
