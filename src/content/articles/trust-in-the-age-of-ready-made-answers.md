The question of trust in science no longer ends with scientists, institutions and media. Increasingly, a language model stands between a person and the knowledge they are trying to reach. It does not merely search, retrieve or summarize. It turns scattered material into a fluent answer. That changes something subtle but important: a person can feel that they know before they have seen the sources, the method, the disagreement and the limits of certainty.

This is not an argument that people have stopped trusting science. It is not an argument that AI has become a new institution of knowledge. It is an essay about the path between not knowing and feeling that one understands. In a search-engine world, that path often passed through titles, links, authors, institutions, publication dates and the comparison of several results. In a model-mediated world, it increasingly starts with a conversation and ends with an answer that feels ready to use.

Prompted Psyche is interested in that moment: not only in the model's answer, but in the psychological shift on the user's side. When the answer is calm, organized and immediate, it becomes harder to notice where helpful orientation ends and where contact with the source has quietly been replaced.

## A crisis of trust sounds simple. The data are more complicated.

Helen Pearson's Nature feature from 1 July 2026 begins with a familiar claim: trust in science is said to be in global crisis. The picture the feature draws is more complicated. It points not to a single collapse, but to differences across countries, topics, social groups, institutions and ways of asking the question. The crisis story has drama. It does not always describe the evidence well.

A large study published in Nature Human Behaviour in 2025, covering 68 countries and more than 71,000 respondents, suggests that trust in scientists remains moderately high in many places. That does not erase the fractures. Health, climate, vaccines, regulation, research funding, political identity and institutional legitimacy all create points of pressure. But the starting point is not simply that "people no longer trust science".

I want to move from that starting point to a different question. This essay is not a section-by-section summary of the Nature article. I treat it as a public trigger for debate: if trust in science is not one simple feeling, what happens when more people encounter science, facts and arguments through a model's answer rather than through direct contact with sources?

That question is not only about whether AI is reliable. It is about where trust is placed. Do we trust the method, the institution, the expert, the author, the citation, the interface or the fluent text that has bundled everything into one convenient response?

## Trust in science is not one feeling

"Trust in science" sounds as if it names a single switch. Trust or distrust. On or off. Real life is messier. People may trust one part of the system and not another. They may trust a physician but distrust a health policy. They may value scientific methods but be skeptical of institutions. They may accept research results while distrusting the way those results are used by media, politicians or platforms.

Trust research often separates dimensions such as competence, honesty, good will, openness and responsibility. Cologna and colleagues, for example, measured trust in scientists through several components, including perceived competence, benevolence, integrity and openness. That is a useful guardrail against flat diagnoses. A person can believe that scientists are competent and still feel that uncertainty is poorly communicated, that institutions are distant or that their own experience is not taken seriously.

This matters in the age of AI because a model often compresses these dimensions into one answer. The user may not see who produced the knowledge, what kind of study is being cited, what disagreement exists, who funded the work, what was left out or where certainty ends. What they do see is a well-formed text. Many separate questions about trust can collapse into one impression: does this sound reasonable?

In everyday use, that compression can be almost invisible. Someone asks about sleep research, social media and teenagers, or the safety of a new technology. The model answers as if one organized expert were speaking. Yet behind a similar answer there may be very different kinds of material: a research review, a single experiment, an institutional report, a media commentary, an industry position or an old fragment of knowledge that needs updating. If we do not separate those layers, we may end up trusting not science, but a smooth blend of elements that carried different weight in their original sources.

That is why [calibrated trust](/concepts/calibrated-trust/) is not general enthusiasm or general suspicion. It is reliance fitted to the task, the stakes, the source base and the possibility of checking. We should read an answer about a term differently from an answer about treatment, climate, legal risk, psychological interpretation or public policy. The tone may be similar. The responsibility is not.

## AI enters between the question and the source

When someone asks a model, they are often no longer asking the source. They are asking an interface that promises a short path from confusion to order. The model responds in natural language, usually in a form shaped around the request: brief, detailed, listed, cautious, expert-like or conversational. This is not merely another channel. It is a different experience of knowing.

Direct contact with sources has friction. A scientific article has structure, methods, tables, limitations and language that may resist easy reading. A report has authors, scope, sponsors, methodological notes and sometimes inconvenient caveats. A book takes time. An expert may say that the matter is more complicated. A bibliography leads onward, but it does not close the question instantly.

A model removes part of that friction. That can be genuinely useful. Someone new to a topic can ask for a map of concepts, a plain-language explanation, a list of points of disagreement or questions to bring back to the sources. But the same convenience can make the path to knowledge less transparent. We see the result of language work. We do not always see the route.

In Prompted Psyche terms, this is a problem of [model output](/concepts/model-output/). A model's answer is a particular kind of text. It is not a raw source. It is not an institution. It is not a study. It is the product of a system that generates language from training, instructions, conversational context and patterns of fit. It can be accurate. It can be useful. It can also sound more grounded than it really is.

## From document to interface

For a long time, the basic image of knowledge was the document. Article, book, report, expert note, institutional page, data table, bibliography. Even when most people did not read full scientific papers, contact with knowledge often carried traces of the document: title, author, source, date, institution, quotation, link.

The conversational interface changes that image. Knowledge appears as an answer to my question. I do not need to begin in a library, database or archive. I do not need to scan a list of search results. I receive a text that seems arranged for me. That changes the relationship. A document that had to be read becomes an answer that can be accepted, refined or simplified.

AI does not only provide content. It formats the relationship with content. It teaches us that knowledge can be conversational, immediate, patient and fitted to our current level. That can be educationally powerful, especially when the original material would otherwise be too costly to approach. At the same time, this format can build trust in the style of the answer rather than in the process that should stand behind it.

When the document remains visible, the reader can at least see that there is an author, genre and institutional setting. When the interface is foregrounded, those elements can become background. The question is not only whether the answer is correct. The question is what had to disappear so the answer could feel this convenient.

## Fluency can imitate credibility

"Sounds good" does not mean "is true." The sentence is simple, but it has to be repeated in work with AI because fluency is one of the strongest traps. A well-structured, calm answer can lower vigilance. A person may not check sources if the text has already given them the feeling of understanding.

Research on processing fluency and truth effects helps name this mechanism carefully. Ease of processing, familiarity of form and a sense of order can influence judgments of truth, but that does not mean every polished text automatically persuades us. In AI use, the more subtle risk is that a fluent answer can reduce the felt need to verify, especially when the path back to the source is not immediately visible.

Language models are especially good at producing a form that resembles explanation. They can organize paragraphs, separate arguments, adopt a careful tone, add conditions and even warn against overconfidence. That is not a flaw in itself. The problem begins when the form of caution replaces real grounding in sources.

Research on hallucination in natural language generation shows that a system can produce fluent, natural and seemingly factual text that is not faithful to the source material. From the user's point of view, the line between a good synthesis and a text that only sounds like a good synthesis is not always visible. A [hallucination](/concepts/hallucination/) is therefore not only a technical failure. It is also a cognitive event for the reader: something that sounds like knowledge may be treated as knowledge.

This is where [algorithmic authority](/concepts/algorithmic-authority/) enters quietly. It does not have to look like naive faith in a machine. It can look ordinary: the model answered quickly, the answer is coherent, I do not have time to check, and the style feels competent. In that moment, authority is not coming from a scientific institution, an author or a method. It is produced by availability, fluency and usefulness.

## What happens to the source when the answer is foregrounded

When the answer is in the foreground, the source can become invisible or secondary. A link may become decoration rather than a condition of credibility. A citation may be treated as a visual sign of seriousness, not as a path back to evidence. The user receives a list of papers but does not open the papers. They see titles but do not check whether the studies exist, what they studied or whether they support the model's claim.

A citation in a model's answer does not end checking. It only shows where checking can begin. A source may exist, but be too general. It may concern a similar topic without supporting the exact sentence. It may also be summarized in a way that hides limits of method, date, scope or disagreement between authors. The better question is not only whether the answer has sources. It is whether those sources really support this specific claim.

The problem is not summarization itself. Summaries are necessary. Good science communication also shortens the path. Teachers, science journalists, physicians, educators and essayists all select, simplify and organize. The difference is that responsible communication shows where claims come from, where simplification happens and what remains unresolved.

AI can support that work, but it does not do so automatically. If the user does not ask for primary sources, does not ask about uncertainty, does not distinguish a study from commentary and does not check whether cited work exists, the answer can replace the route. What emerges is not knowledge, but the feeling of knowledge.

This matters most in high-stakes domains. Health, climate, psychology, law, finance, education, public policy and technology do not require the same level of checking for every small question. They do require awareness of when a model's answer is only the beginning of the work. [Grounding](/concepts/grounding/) is not a decorative feature. It is the condition that lets us move from fluent text to a checkable path.

## AI can also help with the path to knowledge

Criticizing fluent answers is not the same as rejecting AI. Used well, a model can be an excellent guide through difficult material. It can explain concepts, map the structure of an argument, help separate a finding from an interpretation, suggest questions for the author, identify where a primary source is needed and show which parts of an answer are commentary.

AI can also lower the threshold for entering knowledge. A person who does not usually read scientific papers can ask for help with an abstract, the difference between correlation and causation, a short map of positions or a list of terms to look up. That is a real benefit. In this use, AI does not replace the source. It helps someone reach it.

This connects to [cognitive offloading](/concepts/cognitive-offloading/): moving part of cognitive work onto an external tool. We have long done this with notes, calculators, maps, search engines and libraries. AI is another tool, but a more conversational and more suggestive one. It can support attention, but it can also take over too much of the path if the user delegates not only organization, but also the judgment of credibility.

The problem is not AI as an intermediary. The problem is an intermediary that pretends to be the end of the path. If the model helps reveal questions, sources, disagreements and limits, it can strengthen [AI literacy](/concepts/ai-literacy/). If it encourages the user to stop at the first fluent answer, it encourages [overreliance](/concepts/overreliance/).

## A new epistemic vigilance

In the age of AI, [epistemic vigilance](/concepts/epistemic-vigilance/) does not mean paranoia. It does not mean treating every answer as malicious or believing that nothing can be trusted. It means a simpler and more mature habit: how is this known?

That question has several layers. Does the model point to primary or secondary sources? Are the sources current? Is the topic disputed? Does the answer separate what is well established from what is interpretive? Does it show uncertainty? Can I trace a sentence in the answer back to the material on which it should rest?

Epistemic vigilance matters because AI changes the felt effort of checking. In the past, source checking was a separate step. Now we can ask for sources in the same window in which we received the answer. That is convenient, but also risky if the model begins to check itself without contact with external material. "Give me sources" is not enough if we do not verify that the sources exist and say what the answer claims they say.

This new vigilance does not need to be heavy. It can be a small set of good questions asked at the right moment. Not every sentence needs an audit. But answers in high-stakes, disputed or publicly important domains should be traceable back to material. If the path cannot be reconstructed, we do not yet have knowledge. We have a convenient narrative.

## "Trust science" is not enough

"Trust science" can be an understandable response to misinformation, but in a world of intermediaries it may sound like an instruction. For some readers, it does not answer the practical question: whom, exactly, am I being asked to trust? The scientist, the institution, the media outlet, the policy, the platform, the search result, the model or the person speaking in the name of science?

We need a language of process. We need to show how knowledge is made, how confidence changes, how disagreement works, why one study does not close a question, why sources can be ambiguous and what it means for a claim to be better or less well grounded. That does not weaken science. It strengthens it because science is not a magical authority. It is a way of working with evidence, error and correction.

AI can help if we use it to make the process visible. It can generate questions for a text, list assumptions, explain a method, compare positions and identify what is unknown. But if we use it as a machine for final-sounding answers, the process disappears. What remains is an answer that may look like knowledge without a history.

In [Human-AI Interaction](/concepts/human-ai-interaction/), the stakes are therefore not only usability. The deeper question is whether the person preserves cognitive agency. Do they know when they are using help? Do they understand their [mental model](/concepts/mental-model/) of the tool? Can they notice when a model is organizing material and when it is beginning to stand in for the material?

## Calibrated trust means matching reliance to the situation

Research on automation has long distinguished reasonable reliance on a system from reliance that is too strong, too weak or poorly matched to the task. That matters because conversations about AI easily fall into two simple reactions: trust it because the answer is useful, or distrust it because the model can be wrong. Neither reaction is enough.

In practice, the more useful question is calibration. What are the stakes? Is the topic disputed? Would an error have serious consequences? Are sources visible and possible to check? Does the user have enough competence to evaluate the answer, or should they return to a publication, dataset, institution or expert? Those questions help distinguish a situation in which a model can serve as a working guide from one in which it should not be the last word.

## Do not trust the answer. Reconstruct the path.

Mature AI use is not distrust of everything. It is also not trust whenever the model sounds reasonable. It is a different stance: do not trust the answer by itself. Reconstruct the path that the answer should have behind it.

The better question is not only "is the model right?" In many cases, we cannot judge that immediately. The better question is: how did this answer reach me, and can I check that path? Does it lead to sources? Does it show method? Does it reveal disagreement? Does it separate confidence from interpretation? Does it help me go beyond the convenient text?

This is not a small technical habit. It is one of the basic cognitive skills of living with language models. If AI shortens the path to knowledge, people need to know how to lengthen that path again when the situation calls for it. Sometimes a simple answer is enough. Sometimes a report is needed. Sometimes an expert should be consulted. Sometimes the path has to return to the publication, the data or the institution that carries responsibility for the claim.

Trust in the age of AI does not have to mean the end of trust in science. It may mean that trust starts operating in a new place. If it starts at the model's answer, we need to restore the visibility of sources, methods and responsibility. Not to reject AI. To prevent AI from turning the path to knowledge into the mere feeling that we already know.
