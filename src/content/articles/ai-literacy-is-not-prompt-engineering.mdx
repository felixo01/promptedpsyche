AI literacy is often reduced to prompt engineering: knowing how to ask a model for a better answer. That skill matters, but it is only a small surface area of what people need.

The deeper literacy is psychological and organizational. People need to understand when a system is persuasive without being reliable, when fluent output hides uncertainty, and when convenience begins to reshape judgment.

## The problem with prompt-first thinking

Prompt-first thinking treats AI as a vending machine for better outputs. It asks whether the instruction is good enough, not whether the task should be delegated, how the answer should be checked, or what new dependency is being created.

AI literacy should help people notice the whole interaction: the goal, the model, the user, the context, the verification path and the downstream effects.

## A more useful definition

AI literacy is the ability to work with intelligent systems while preserving human judgment. It includes:

- knowing what the system can and cannot infer
- recognizing overconfidence in both machine output and human interpretation
- choosing when to rely, verify, revise or refuse
- understanding how AI changes communication and responsibility

Prompt craft belongs inside that frame. It should not replace it.

## Why this matters

Organizations that train people only to prompt better may create faster workflows and weaker judgment. The real goal is not more polished output. The real goal is better human-AI decisions.
